The proposition: Seam-ful AI for learning

HelpMe can't out-muscle ChatGPT or Claude, and it shouldn't try. The big models are built to feel seamless — easy to trust, easy to hand your thinking over to. But a course is optimised for understanding, not output, and for that a tool you can see through beats a more capable one you can't.

Legible failure can be a very good teacher. Let's identify the friction in the system that maps onto a real mechanism. RAG matters because retrieval is a layer whose seams you can actually inspect — the chunks, the citations, what got pulled and what got left out. You can't see through a model's weights, but you can see through retrieval. HelpMe's silent failures are visible weaknesses, and visible weakness is where understanding gets built — judgment you then carry into the seamless tools whose weaknesses and silent failures won't be as visible. Build skills here that can be used elsewhere.

The one idea underneath everything: HelpMe does not read whole papers. Ahead of time, each reading is split into short passages — chunks — and an embedding model turns every chunk into a vector, a string of numbers that captures its meaning, stored in a database. When you prompt it, your words are embedded into a vector too, and the system runs a semantic search for the chunks whose vectors sit closest to yours. Only the top handful are placed in the context window with your prompt, and the model writes its answer from that. Anything not retrieved stays invisible to it — which is why some of your sources never appear, and why rewording your prompt changes what comes back.

Pick a query and watch which sources light up. Slide the top-k. Nothing chooses to leave a source out — its chunks just rank too low to be pulled in. When a relevant one stays dark, that is a silent failure: the answer was in the library, but the model never saw it — and it will still answer confidently without it.

The gap it can't retrieve. It only ever sees a few snippets, never the whole picture, so it is weakest at “what is missing?” questions — the answer is the space between sources, and no single chunk contains it.

Needle vs. haystack. A smaller top-k hands the model just the likely needles; a larger one adds more hay it can lose the answer in.

HelpMe pulls a few chunks. The tools you use outside class increasingly take the whole text in one giant context window instead. Same goal, different failure modes — and that choice is a real engineering decision, not just clunky vs. slick.

Retrieval — what HelpMe does

Strengths
  • Scales to a huge, shared, always-updating library.
  • Cheap per question — it reads only a few passages.
  • Returns citations you can trace back to a source.
Weaknesses
  • Can miss or mis-rank a source — the silent failure in panel 1.
  • Only ever sees the few chunks it pulled.
  • Quality depends on how you word the query.

Long context — paste it all in

Strengths
  • Sees the whole text at once — no retrieval misses.
  • Can reason across a full document, and spot what is missing.
  • No vector database to build or maintain.
Weaknesses
  • A hard limit on how much text fits.
  • Costs more as you add text, and runs slower.
  • “Lost in the middle” — it under-uses material buried mid-prompt, and gives no automatic citations.

The point is not which wins — it is that each fails differently. HelpMe's missed sources are the price of a shared, citable, ever-growing library; pasting everything into a chatbot trades that for a size cap, higher cost, and the lost-in-the-middle problem.
